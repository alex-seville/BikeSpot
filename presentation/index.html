Bike Parking

Our use case was:

Bob is leaving his house to meet a friend at a restaurant in San Francisco.

Before he leaves (or when he arrives) he wants to make sure there is reliable bike parking near the restaurant.

Bike Parking

To accomplish that, we developed a map-based application that allows you to:

  • search for a destination
  • view nearby bike racks
  • ensure there is a bike rack within a reasonable walking distance of your destination
  • and add bike racks that are missing from our service

Technical Challenges

We addressed the following challenges

  • Customizing map annotations
  • Camera customization
  • Hierarchy of Views
  • Sourcing of data

Customizing map annotations

The default MKPinAnnotation comes in 3 colors: red, green, purple.

Apple prescribes usages to each of these colors, and doesn't allow you to specify colors manually without adding your own image assets or (like we did) finding a Pod that redraws the pins with your specified color.

Camera customization

The defauly camera control in iOS, as we learned in class, cannot easily be customized.

It is easy to use, and works for our app at this stage, but customizing the color to match our theme would have required implementing things manually.

Hierarchy of Views

The map exposed a variety of gestures, so determining the correct view hierarchy was something that took us time to figure out.

Eventually we chose to manage all views from a single parent, and communicate using NSNotifications.

Sourcing of Data

When we started the project we assumed we would have to manually add data, or generate dummy data to have a demo.

Luckily, the City of San Francisco not only provides bike rack data for unlimited free public use, but they also furnished us with an updated corpus.
